# The Case for Relevnt: Why Career Intelligence Is the Infrastructure Humanity Needs

## Executive Summary

The labor market is structurally broken. Job seekers submit hundreds of applications into a void where 30% of postings are ghost jobs, response rates hover near 3%, and the psychological toll drives clinical-level depression. Employers, meanwhile, spend $240,000 per bad hire and report record difficulty filling roles. The gap between these two realities is not a staffing problem — it is an information architecture failure. Relevnt exists to close that gap by building an AI-powered career intelligence platform that aggregates job data, company intelligence, and market signals into a single, transparent system that serves both job seekers and recruiters. This report presents the research-grounded case for why Relevnt should exist, why it represents a compelling investment opportunity, and why it is fundamentally good for humankind.

***

## The Crisis: A Job Market Failing Everyone

### Job Seekers Are Drowning

The modern job search has become a full-time job of its own — one that pays nothing and erodes the mental health of those trapped in it. In 2025, job seekers submitted between 32 and 200+ applications on average before receiving a single offer, with cold online applications yielding a success rate of just 0.1% to 2%. LinkedIn's response rate for applicants was a mere 3.1%, while Indeed performed only slightly better at 4.5%. The median time to a first offer jumped from 57 days in Q1 2025 to a punishing 83 days by Q4.[^1][^2]

These numbers obscure a deeper cruelty. A Forbes investigation of Bureau of Labor Statistics data found that in June 2025 alone, employers reported 7.4 million job openings but made only 5.2 million hires — leaving more than 2.2 million ghost jobs that wasted job seekers' time. Greenhouse's own platform analysis found that 18–22% of all job postings are ghost jobs, with the figure reaching 31% in corporate services. The BBC reported that as many as 22% of online job postings had no genuine intent to hire. Regulatory bodies have begun responding: the FTC established a Joint Labor Task Force in February 2025 to tackle misleading job advertisements, and California introduced legislation requiring employers to disclose whether a posting corresponds to an actual vacancy.[^3][^4][^5]

### Employers Are Bleeding Capital

The crisis cuts both ways. According to SHRM's 2025 Talent Trends data, nearly 7 in 10 organizations still report difficulty filling roles, with 51% citing low applicant numbers, 50% facing stiff competition, and 41% dealing with candidate ghosting — the same top three challenges reported three years running. A Robert Half survey found that 58% of business leaders said finding skilled talent is more difficult than a year ago, with 65% now reporting specific challenges tied to AI-generated applications flooding their pipelines.[^6][^7]

The cost of getting it wrong is staggering. The U.S. Department of Labor estimates a bad hire costs up to 30% of the employee's first-year earnings. Industry experts put the true cost at up to $240,000 per bad hire when factoring in all indirect costs and cascading effects. CareerBuilder found that 75% of companies have been impacted by a bad hire, with 41% reporting losses exceeding $25,000 per incident. Gallup estimates that actively disengaged employees — often the product of poor matches — cost U.S. businesses between $450 and $550 billion annually in lost productivity.[^8][^9]

### The Information Asymmetry at the Root

The fundamental issue is not a shortage of jobs or talent — it is an information architecture failure. A Columbia University field experiment with 1,100 graduating students demonstrated that prior to entering the labor market, job seekers have wildly imprecise beliefs about available wages, with errors ranging from -98% underestimation to +400% overestimation. When given accurate information, students adjusted their reservation wages, and each 1% increase via the information channel led to a 1.28% increase in total compensation upon employment. The study also found that information intervention compressed wage dispersion, suggesting that better data leads to fairer outcomes.[^10]

A recent Indeed survey crystallized the disconnect: 70% of employers say they understand their workforce, but only 18% of job seekers agree. This is not a communication gap — it is a structural failure of the platforms that mediate between the two sides.[^11]

## The Human Cost: Why This Is a Moral Imperative

### Mental Health Destruction

The psychological toll of the broken job market is clinically significant. A systematic review of 33 longitudinal studies found that sudden job loss or continuous unemployment is associated with elevated depression, psychological distress, and anxiety. Analyses of state unemployment rates suggest a direct link between unemployment and suicide. Research published in PMC found that unemployment is significantly associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, and bipolar disorder.[^12][^13]

The damage is compounding: adults who lost jobs and subsequently became depressed had 67% lower odds of reemployment within four years compared to those with low depressive symptoms. The American Psychological Association warns that "losing a job and being unemployed for a long period of time is a psychological trauma and a financial trauma, and the two are closely intertwined". In 2025, 72% of job seekers reported that the search process negatively impacted their mental health.[^14][^15][^12]

This creates a vicious cycle: job loss causes depression, depression prevents reemployment, prolonged unemployment deepens depression. Any platform that can shorten the search, reduce wasted effort on ghost jobs, and provide real intelligence rather than false hope is not merely a business — it is a mental health intervention.

### Racial and Socioeconomic Inequity

The broken system does not fail everyone equally. A landmark National Bureau of Economic Research study — the largest of its kind — sent 80,000 fake résumés to apply for 10,000 jobs at 100 major companies. Employers were 9.5% more likely to call back applicants with white-sounding names than those with Black-sounding names. One-fifth of companies, predominantly in retail and automotive, accounted for nearly half of the total racial discrimination measured. A separate review of all available audit studies found that discrimination against Black applicants had not changed in three decades.[^16][^17]

The Economic Policy Institute notes that the 2-to-1 disparity in unemployment between Black and white workers is "one of the most durable and defining features of the U.S. labor market". More than 70% of first-generation college students miss out on internship opportunities, and nearly half of college graduates are underemployed. These are not individual failures — they are systemic outcomes of a system that distributes opportunity through networks, credentials, and opaque processes rather than through skills and potential.[^18][^19]

A career intelligence platform that surfaces verified opportunity data, exposes company-level hiring patterns, and matches on skills rather than pedigree is inherently an equity tool. It provides the kind of information that privileged job seekers get through personal networks — and makes it available to everyone.

## The Market Opportunity: Why Investors Should Pay Attention

### A Multi-Billion-Dollar Category in Expansion

The HR technology market reached $47.51 billion in 2026 and is forecast to grow at a 10.35% CAGR through 2031. S&P Global's 451 Research valued the broader HR technology market at $94 billion, driven by hybrid work, skills-first workforce planning, and demand for personalized engagement. Cloud-based platforms account for 88.2% of revenue and are advancing at a 12.56% CAGR. People analytics specifically — the segment most aligned with Relevnt's core intelligence layer — is forecast at a 12.4% CAGR, driven by predictive insights on attrition, productivity, and workforce optimization.[^20][^21]

### The Skills Gap Creates Structural Demand

The global skills gap could result in $8.5 trillion in unrealized annual revenues by 2030, according to Korn Ferry. IDC estimates that over 90% of global enterprises will face critical skills shortages by 2026, with sustained gaps risking $5.5 trillion in losses from product delays, quality issues, and missed revenue. In the U.S. alone, the skills gap could leave a $2.5 trillion impact over the next decade. The World Economic Forum found that even one digital skill boosts an employee's earnings by 23%, and three or more can increase wages by 45%.[^22][^23][^24]

These numbers represent the addressable pain. Any platform that helps employers find the right talent faster and helps workers identify and close skills gaps is positioned to capture a share of trillions in economic value currently being lost to inefficiency.

### Validation from Major Players

The opportunity is being validated by some of the world's most influential organizations. The Schultz Family Foundation committed over $3 million in 2025 to support AI-powered career navigation tools across four organizations, explicitly aiming to "close the opportunity gap" for underserved populations. OpenAI announced a Jobs Platform in partnership with companies including Walmart, John Deere, Accenture, and Indeed, positioning AI-driven career tools as a core pillar of economic opportunity. Brookings Institution published research advocating for hybrid AI systems that combine structured taxonomies with adaptive ontologies for more inclusive, skills-based job matching — precisely the architecture Relevnt is building.[^25][^26][^27]

LinkedIn's own data shows that a skills-based hiring approach increases the talent pipeline by 8.2x in AI roles, and 75% of HR professionals believe skills-based hiring improves workforce diversity and quality simultaneously. Skills-based methods also reduce turnover by up to 30%. The market is moving toward what Relevnt envisions — and platforms that build the intelligence infrastructure early will have the strongest position.[^28][^29]

## Why Relevnt Specifically

### Intelligence Over Aggregation

Most job platforms are marketplaces: they list jobs, collect applications, and let volume do the work. This approach produced the crisis described above. Relevnt's architecture — with AI-powered job ingestion, company data aggregation, and recruiter/seeker dual-sided intelligence — represents a fundamentally different model. Instead of more noise, it provides signal: verified, enriched data about companies, roles, markets, and fit.

This is aligned with the Columbia research showing that information itself changes outcomes. When job seekers have accurate data about market conditions, they make better decisions, earn higher wages, and experience less wage dispersion. When employers have richer candidate intelligence, they reduce the $240,000 cost of bad hires. Relevnt is not another job board — it is the intelligence layer that job boards were supposed to be.[^10][^8]

### Dual-Sided Value Creation

Relevnt serves both job seekers and recruiters, creating network effects that single-sided platforms cannot achieve. For job seekers, it provides company intelligence, market signals, and AI-driven matching that democratizes the kind of career navigation previously available only through expensive coaches or elite networks. For recruiters, it provides enriched candidate profiles and data-driven hiring intelligence that reduces time-to-fill and improves match quality.

This dual-sided approach addresses the core finding from Indeed's research: the 52-point perception gap between employers and job seekers about mutual understanding. By making both sides more transparent and data-rich, Relevnt doesn't just serve a market — it fixes the structural mismatch that defines it.[^11]

### Technical Credibility and Builder Signal

The codebase audit reveals a platform built with production-grade architecture: React, Tailwind CSS, Supabase, AI/ML integrations, and serverless functions — a modern, scalable stack that signals technical maturity and engineering discipline. The fact that this system is being built with an investor-grade intelligence extraction methodology demonstrates a builder who thinks about investability from the architecture up, not as an afterthought.

## The Thesis: Why This Matters for Humanity

The job market is not merely inefficient — it is a system that actively harms the people it is supposed to serve. It wastes the time of 7.2 million Americans applying to jobs that don't exist. It drives clinical depression in 72% of job seekers. It discriminates against Black applicants at rates unchanged in 30 years. It costs employers up to $240,000 per mismatch. And it leaves $8.5 trillion in potential economic value unrealized due to skills gaps.[^5][^15][^8][^22][^16]

Career intelligence — the kind Relevnt is building — is not a luxury feature for the already-privileged. It is infrastructure. It is the informational scaffolding that enables a 22-year-old first-generation college student in Iowa to see the same opportunities, understand the same market dynamics, and make the same caliber of career decisions as a Stanford graduate with a network of family connections. It is the transparency layer that forces companies to post real jobs, treat candidates with respect, and compete on the quality of their culture rather than the opacity of their process.

The Schultz Family Foundation, OpenAI, and Brookings are all betting that AI-powered career intelligence is the next critical piece of economic infrastructure. The research is unambiguous: better information leads to better wages, shorter searches, improved mental health outcomes, and more equitable access to opportunity. Relevnt is positioned to deliver exactly that — for everyone, at scale.[^26][^27][^25]
